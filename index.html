Research

Resource Misallocation in the R&D Sector: Evidence from China (Job Market Paper)

Factor market wedges can lead to large distortions in the R&D sector, resulting in slower productivity growth. To show this, I develop an endogenous growth model with heterogeneous factor market access. The wedge lowers welfare in two ways. First, the wedge leads to resource misallocation in the production sector, which lowers the productivity level. Second, the wedge distorts return to R&D, resulting in resource misallocation in the R&D sector and lower productivity growth. I apply the model to China, where political connections grant state-owned firms better factor market access. Results show that state-owned firms are inefficient in producing innovation. Nevertheless, they invest more in R&D than private firms because of their higher expected return. This leads to misallocation in the R&D sector. Removing state-owned firms’ privileged market access increases annual productivity growth by 1.2 percentage points and total welfare by 23%. A decomposition of the welfare effect shows dynamic loss from lower growth rate accounts for 90% of the welfare loss.


Political Connection, Financial Frictions, and Allocative Efficiency (draft coming soon)

I estimate the effect of 2004 Chinese banking reform on the allocative efficiency of credit. Using exogenous variations in the density of politically connected firms resulting from a high-profile national defense project from 1960s, I find the banking reform reduces TFPR dispersion more in cities with more connected firms. Furthermore, the results are driven by better access to credit by politically unconnected firms, who lacks access to credit prior to the reform. With the newly acquired credit, these unconnected firms invest more and grow faster. A back-of-the-envelope calculation shows the banking reform improves the allocative efficiency by over 20% (measured using TFPR dispersion).
